To my colleagues in journalism education:
 

Free speech in school. It sounds great — and it is — but without students standing up for their rights, students wouldn’t be able to express themselves as freely as they can today.


K-State is bringing to campus one of the people who opened the door to student free speech. When Mary Beth Tinker wore a black arm band to her high school in Des Moines, her principal told her should wasn’t allowed to wear it. But she persisted, and her case went to the U.S. Supreme Court, which decided in Tinker vs. Des Moines Independent School District that high school students have a First Amendment right to free speech.


Mary Beth is coming to K-State on Saturday, Feb. 24, to share her story. She is an outgoing and engaging speaker who easily connects with students.


Your students, faculty and friends are invited to her public presentation, at 2:30 in the K-State Student Union. You and your students are invited to a special pizza lunch for high school students before the presentation, where Los Angeles artist Linda Pollack will help explain why free speech matters in a democracy. The pizza is free if you send an email with the number of students who will attend. We’ll host it in the Beach Museum of Art on the K-State campus, but please note that our capacity for the lunch is 80, so please register soon by emailing Kathrine Walker Schlageck at klwalk@ksu.edu.


I hope you’ll join me in this exciting chance to hear how one high schooler stood up for her rights, all the way to the Supreme Court, and won!


Steve Wolgast

wolgast@ksu.edu

Professor of practice, A. Q. Miller School of Journalism and Mass Communications
